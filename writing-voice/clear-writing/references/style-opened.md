# Style Reference: OpenEd Voice

Extracted from Charlie's published OpenEd Daily and Weekly newsletters. This is the house voice for OpenEd content - Pirate Wires register applied to education.

**Use for:** OpenEd newsletters, blog posts, social content, any education-focused writing.

---

## Voice Patterns (From Charlie's Published Writing)

### Rhythm

- Long factual/narrative sentences (30-40 words) followed by short punches (4-8 words)
- "I mean, obviously..." before stating what should be self-evident
- Parenthetical asides that say what everyone's thinking: "(Rhetorical question. We all know why.)"
- Let the quote do the work. Don't editorialize after a strong quote.

### Tone

- **Curious > Accusatory** - Notice gaps, don't blame
- **Confident** - State opinions as observations, not suggestions
- **Specific** - Real names, real numbers, real sources
- **"I just noticed this" energy** - Write like you're thinking in real time, not presenting a finished argument

### Witty Voice Levels

- **High wit:** Opening letters, segment titles, THOUGHT segments, social media
- **Medium wit:** TREND segments, tool introductions
- **Low wit:** TOOL descriptions, hyperlinked references, CTAs

## Copywork

### Data vs. Headlines (Calling Out the Obvious)

> Homeschool students outperform public school peers in science, math, history, English, the ACT, and the SAT.
>
> He's citing data from HSLDA's 2009 Progress Report, a nationwide study of nearly 12,000 homeschooled students conducted by Dr. Brian D. Ray. That study found homeschoolers scoring 34-39 percentile points above national averages. More than 78% of peer-reviewed studies since have shown the same pattern, and the advantage holds regardless of family income or parental education level.
>
> I mean, this isn't obscure research. It's been replicated for decades. So why doesn't the coverage match the data? (Rhetorical question. We all know why.)

**What works:** Long factual sentence, then short punch. The "I mean" before stating what should be obvious. Parenthetical that says what everyone's thinking.

### Institutional Critique (Gatto Deep Dive)

> In 1991, John Taylor Gatto did something that shocked the education world. Just months after being named New York State Teacher of the Year, he quit. Not quietly into retirement, but loudly, with a scathing *Wall Street Journal* op-ed announcing: "I can't teach this way any longer. If you hear of a job where I don't have to hurt kids to make a living, let me know."
>
> "Schooling is a form of adoption," Gatto later wrote. "You give your kid away at his most plastic years to a group of strangers."

**What works:** Narrative setup with specific detail (1991, NYS Teacher of the Year). The quote does the heavy lifting - no need to editorialize.

### The Reframe (Design Over Delivery)

> There's a question parents keep asking about online learning: "Is it good or bad?"
>
> Wrong question.
>
> Michael Horn took two online courses at Harvard. Same platform. Same student. Same year. One was brilliant - compelling storylines, short bursts of text, instructor engagement when he needed it. The other was a wall of dense paragraphs with no support. He could barely stay awake.
>
> The variable wasn't "online." It was design.

**What works:** Sets up the common framing, kills it with two words ("Wrong question."), then proves it with a specific anecdote. The last line is a tight reframe.

### Tool Segment with Personality

> You're probably hemorrhaging money on printer ink right now. Most homeschool families are.
>
> The math is brutal: that $50 inkjet printer costs $500 a year in cartridges. Meanwhile, an ink tank printer costs more upfront but pennies per page. Do the math over three years and the "expensive" printer saves you hundreds.
>
> The rule: never buy cartridges again. Ink tanks or laser. Everything else is a trap.

**What works:** Direct address ("You're probably..."), concrete math, ends with a firm opinion stated as fact. No hedging, no "consider" or "might want to."

### Opening Letter with Hook

> There's a question hiding under a lot of today's education coverage:
>
> **Can parents be trusted?**
>
> Every time a big outlet runs a story about a homeschooled student who "fell behind," the implication is the same: when families are left to themselves, kids get shortchanged.
>
> These pieces follow a familiar pattern. Spotlight an anecdote. Quietly generalize it into a warning label. Skip the part that would actually help readers think clearly: what the broader data says.

**What works:** Opens with a quiet observation, escalates to the bold question, then dissects the media pattern. Confident, analytical, not angry.

## Opening Letter Patterns (5 Types)

### 1. Milestone/Community Celebrations

> "Greetings Eddies! It just came to my attention that the OpenEd Daily hit a new milestone: 20,000 subscribers! Rather than resting on our laurels..."

### 2. Contrarian/Data Hooks

> "Greetings Eddies! I drafted today's 'Trend' segment late last week, praising the New York Times... Then, yesterday, they published a guest essay with a very different perspective."

### 3. Story-Driven Openings

> "I have a confession to make. I was homeschooled (no, that's not the confession). The confession is that between ages 4 and 13, roughly half my time was spent playing with LEGO."

> "Growing up, my mom would always say, 'You boys should take a small engine repair class.' I never did and I still don't know how to repair small engines."

### 4. Personal/Humorous Tone

> "Greetings Eddies! Did you know that OpenEd is on Instagram? While I may not be able to compete with top-tier influencer homeschool moms, we're always looking for new ways to share the message (even if that means putting on a smelly latex horse mask and A/B testing silly gimmicks until we find something that sticks)."

### 5. Parental Anxiety Hooks

> "Greetings! We've all seen it, felt the pressure, and asked ourselves: *Am I doing enough for my child?*"

**Signature Greetings:** "Greetings Eddies!", "Welcome Eddies!", "Happy Monday!", or just "Greetings!"

## THOUGHT vs TREND (Critical Distinction)

A THOUGHT is a position. It has a thesis - an opinion the writer is willing to defend. The first sentence should state the take. Everything after is evidence.

A TREND is a fact. It reports what happened, cites the data, attributes the source. No thesis required.

If your THOUGHT reads like a report of what happened without stating what you think about it, it's a TREND wearing a THOUGHT label. Add the take or reclassify it.

## Segment Archetypes

### THOUGHT Archetypes

1. **The Parable** - Story with embedded lesson. Reader discovers insight through narrative.
2. **The Vivid Analogy** - Maps education concepts onto familiar objects.
3. **The Subversive Reframe** - Concede conventional view, then flip it.
4. **The Gut-Punch Quote** - Let someone else deliver the insight.
5. **The One-Liner** - Single sentence that reframes everything. Rare. Devastating when right.

### TREND Archetypes

1. **Alarming Stat + Human Voice** - Data creates urgency; human story creates connection.
2. **Follow the Smart Money** - What are sophisticated actors actually doing?
3. **Historical Parallel** - Today's trend maps onto past movements.
4. **Concrete Case Study** - One family, one school, one district - told in detail.

### TOOL Archetypes

1. **How-To in Disguise** - Don't describe the tool, show how to use it.
2. **Personal Endorsement** - You or named source vouches for it.
3. **Problem-Solution Frame** - Name the pain point first.
4. **"We Tried This" Endorsement** - Team/community validation.

## Segment Titles

- Format: `# TYPE: TITLE` (H1 header)
- Types: THOUGHT, TOOL, TREND
- 1-6 words, ALL CAPS after type prefix
- Create information gap - reader must read to understand

### Witty Title Patterns

- **Pun on Familiar Phrase:** "Home Is Where the School Is"
- **Cultural Reference:** "If You Build It, They Will Learn"
- **Tautology That Makes a Point:** "The Business of Education"
- **Dramatic Framing of Mundane:** "The Great Curriculum Wars"

## 7 Pirate Wires Techniques for Education Segments

1. **Calling Out the Obvious** - Say what everyone knows but won't admit about education
2. **The "You're the Problem" Flip** - Show how complainers are causing the issue
3. **The Absurdity Framework** - Take something overblown, describe with exaggerated gravity
4. **Setup and Punch** - Serious setup, unexpected punch line
5. **The "Let's Be Honest" Approach** - Cut through education BS
6. **Institutional Critique** - Question why institutions act the way they do (without preaching)
7. **The Ironic Contrast** - Show gap between what's prioritized and what actually matters

**Parenthetical Asides (1-2 per segment max):**
- "(cue the socialization concern)" - Dismissal
- "(thank you, science)" - Sarcastic credit
- "(from best I can tell, [observation])" - Honest aside
- "#Just[Place]Things" - Hashtag joke

**Casual Interjections (sparingly):**
- "lol" - After absurd observation
- "Look:" - Before real-talk moment
- "I mean," - Before obvious point

## Anti-Patterns (OpenEd-Specific)

- Staccato marketing fragments: "100,000 tutors. 90+ languages. Results." - write flowing sentences
- Correlative constructions: "X isn't just Y - it's Z" - find another way
- Fake questions: "What if schools actually taught kids to think?" - just tell them
- Hedging: "might," "could," "possibly" - be confident
- Over-editorializing after quotes - let the quote land
- Performative emotional narration: "X said something that stopped me" / "this hit me hard" - drop the quote and let it land
- Force wit where substance needed
- Jokes about sensitive family situations
- Snark without a point
- Clever at the expense of clarity

## Newsletter Structure

### Daily (Mon-Thu): Thought-Trend-Tool

500-800 words. THOUGHT → TREND → TOOL, each ~100-120 words:
- **THOUGHT:** Contrarian takes, educational philosophy
- **TREND:** Current developments, research, data (must be data-driven, not just stories)
- **TOOL:** Practical resources readers can use immediately (lead with BENEFIT, not features)

### Weekly (Friday): Full Digest

1,500-2,500 words. Sections:
- Opening Letter (no header - launch straight into content)
- Featured Podcast (1 compelling moment, not overview)
- Deep Dive (3-4 paragraphs, story-driven, third-person, pacing: Story → Problem → Data → Silver Lining)
- Trends We're Following (data-driven items with source attribution)
- Tools We're Bookmarking (benefit-first, include price when relevant)
- From the Community (optional)
- Ed's Stable (closing synthesis)

"Pirate Wires-influenced voice" is the explicit style directive.

## Formatting Rules (Critical)

- NO EMOJIS in newsletter body (non-negotiable)
- Images ABOVE H1 section titles, not below
- H1 for all sections (not H2)
- Opening letter has no header - just launch in
- Include links early in opening letter (many readers won't scroll past)
- Source attribution: put source name EARLY in text with hyperlink, not parenthetical at end
- McGarry-style link format for Best Links: Single curiosity-provoking sentence + hyperlinked source in parens. No bold, no italics on description text.

### Tools Format

Per item: Tool Name (hyperlinked) + benefit-first description (1-2 sentences) + source attribution (parentheses). Lead with what it DOES, not what it IS. NO category grouping - list sequentially. Max ONE Reddit post.

### Trends Format

Per item: Headline (hyperlinked) + context/data (1-2 sentences) + source attribution (parentheses). Must be data-driven. Specific numbers when available.

---

## Substance → Take Rhythm

The pacing alternates: Substance (facts, quotes, data) → Take (interpretation, implication) → Substance → Take. This is the core engine of the OpenEd voice - facts earn the right to opinions.
