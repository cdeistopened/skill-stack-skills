# Book Titler

Generate titles and subtitles for nonfiction books optimized for both human curiosity and Amazon discoverability.

## Core Principle: Subtitle First, Title Second

This is Joshua Lisec's key insight, and it contradicts how most authors think. The subtitle does the selling. The title does the stopping.

**Why subtitle first:** The subtitle carries the promises — what the reader gets. Once you know what you're promising, you can craft a title that makes a stranger curious enough to click. Working title-first leads to clever-but-empty titles that don't convert.

---

## The Job of Each Component

| Component | Job | Analogy |
|-----------|-----|---------|
| **Title** | Stop the scroll. Create curiosity. Make someone say "Huh, that sounds interesting." | Billboard headline |
| **Subtitle** | Deliver 3 promises. Tell the reader what they'll get. Carry the keywords. | The pitch |
| **Cover** | Signal genre, quality, professionalism. Reinforce the title. | The handshake |

The 6-step buying sequence (Lisec): Stop scrolling → Click → "This looks cool" → Read description → Read sample → Buy. The title's ONLY job is steps 1-2.

---

## Step 1: Define the 3 Promises (Subtitle Work)

Before writing a single title candidate, articulate what the book delivers. Use Lisec's 3-promise framework:

| Promise Type | What It Answers | Example (Fitness Book) |
|-------------|----------------|----------------------|
| **Generic Functional** | "What will I learn/be able to do?" | Build strength without a gym |
| **Specific Functional** | "What's the concrete method/system?" | The 15-minute bodyweight protocol |
| **Generic Emotional** | "How will I feel?" | Confidence in your own skin |

**Process:**
1. List every benefit the reader gets from the book (brainstorm 15-20)
2. Group them into functional vs. emotional
3. Pick the strongest from each category
4. Draft 5-10 subtitle candidates that weave all 3 promises together

**Subtitle formula patterns that work:**
- `How to [Specific Functional] So You Can [Emotional]` — "How to Build a 15-Minute Fitness Habit So You Can Feel Strong at Any Age"
- `The [Method] for [Audience] Who Want [Outcome]` — "The Busy Professional's System for Losing Weight Without Dieting"
- `[Number] [Framework] to [Outcome] — Even If [Objection]` — "7 Protocols to Sleep Through the Night — Even If You've Tried Everything"
- `[Specific Functional]: [Generic Functional] and [Emotional]` — "Practical Strategies to Build Lasting Wealth and Financial Peace of Mind"

---

## Step 2: Keyword Research (Before Titling)

Your subtitle is your strongest SEO asset. Keywords in title/subtitle rank 37% higher than identical keywords in backend boxes (Dave Chesson, 121-author experiment).

**Process:**
1. **Amazon autocomplete:** Type your topic, note every suggestion. Use alphabet technique (topic + a, topic + b, etc.)
2. **Publisher Rocket / DataForSEO:** Get search volume for candidate phrases
3. **Competitor mining:** Find self-published books with BSR < 100,000. Browse author pages for validated keywords (Kings of Kindle method)
4. **Pick 1-2 primary keywords** that MUST appear in subtitle
5. **Pick 2-3 secondary keywords** for backend boxes

**Rules:**
- Primary keyword belongs in the subtitle, not forced into the title
- Never sacrifice readability for keyword stuffing
- Don't duplicate subtitle keywords in backend boxes (wastes indexing)
- Subtitle keywords should sound natural when read aloud

## Step 3: Title Generation (Curiosity Mechanisms)

Now that you know what you're promising (subtitle) and what people search for (keywords), generate the title. The title's job is CURIOSITY, not clarity. The subtitle handles clarity.

### 9 Title Types for Nonfiction

Synthesized from Tucker Max/Scribe Media, Cascadia Author Services, World Changing Books, and bestseller analysis:

| # | Type | Pattern | Example |
|---|------|---------|---------|
| 1 | **Big Idea / Concept** | Name your method or framework | *Atomic Habits*, *Deep Work*, *Essentialism* |
| 2 | **Provocative / Contrarian** | Challenge conventional wisdom | *The Subtle Art of Not Giving a F*ck*, *So Good They Call You a Fake* |
| 3 | **Promise / Outcome** | State the transformation | *Never Split the Difference*, *How to Win Friends and Influence People* |
| 4 | **Paradox / Impossible Claim** | Juxtapose contradictions | *The 4-Hour Workweek*, *Effortless* |
| 5 | **Metaphor / Analogy** | Borrow imagery from another domain | *Blue Ocean Strategy*, *The Tipping Point* |
| 6 | **Question** | Pose the question the book answers | *Who Moved My Cheese?*, *What Got You Here Won't Get You There* |
| 7 | **Number / List** | Use a number for specificity | *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People*, *The 5 AM Club* |
| 8 | **Audience-Named** | Call out who it's for | *Lean In: Women, Work, and the Will to Lead* |
| 9 | **Descriptive / Direct** | Say what it is (instructional books) | *How to Read a Book*, *On Writing* |

**The disagreement:** Tucker Max and Joshua Lisec both say curiosity > clarity in titles. Dave Chesson and KDP Kings say keywords > curiosity because Amazon search drives discovery. **The resolution:** Use a curiosity-driven title + keyword-rich subtitle. This gives you both. Lisec's approach and Chesson's data are not in conflict — they optimize different components.

### Title Constraints (Data-Driven)

From World Changing Books bestseller analysis (95% of NYT bestsellers share these traits):

- **1-6 syllables ideal** (85% of bestsellers). Max 10 syllables (99% of bestsellers).
- **Easy to spell** — the "Google factor." If someone can't spell it, they can't search for it.
- **Easy to say** — word-of-mouth depends on pronounceability.
- **Unique / not already taken** — search Amazon and Google. A duplicate title kills discoverability.
- **No special characters or punctuation** that create search inconsistencies (Greenleaf Book Group).
- **Works at thumbnail size** — Derek Murphy: title should fill ~100% width of the cover for unknown authors.

### Title + Subtitle Length

- **Amazon limit:** 200 characters total (title + subtitle)
- **Optimal display:** 60-80 characters total (beyond this, Amazon truncates in search results)
- **Title:** 2-6 words / 1-6 syllables
- **Subtitle:** 8-15 words

## Step 4: Evaluate Candidates

### The 10-Point Title Checklist

Score each candidate 0 or 1 on each criterion:

| # | Criterion | Source |
|---|-----------|--------|
| 1 | **Curiosity:** Would a stranger say "Huh, that sounds interesting"? | Lisec |
| 2 | **Brevity:** Title is 1-6 syllables / 2-6 words? | Bestseller data |
| 3 | **Speakability:** Easy to say in conversation and remember? | World Changing Books |
| 4 | **Spellability:** Easy to type into a search bar? | World Changing Books |
| 5 | **Uniqueness:** No competing books with same or very similar title? | Chesson |
| 6 | **Genre signal:** Does it feel like a [self-help / business / health] book? | Derek Murphy |
| 7 | **Subtitle carries keywords:** Primary search term is in the subtitle? | Chesson / KDP Kings |
| 8 | **Subtitle delivers 3 promises:** Generic functional + specific functional + emotional? | Lisec |
| 9 | **Thumbnail test:** Title readable at Amazon thumbnail size (~120px wide)? | Murphy |
| 10 | **No confusion:** Title + subtitle together make the topic immediately clear? | Tucker Max |

**Scoring:** 8-10 = strong candidate. 6-7 = needs work. Below 6 = restart.

## Step 5: Test Before Publishing

### Methods (Ranked by Signal Quality)

| Method | Cost | Time | Signal |
|--------|------|------|--------|
| **PickFu poll** | $15-50 | 15 min | Best. Real audience votes + written explanations. Test up to 8 titles. Target by demographics. |
| **Google/Facebook Ads** | $20-50 | 2-5 days | Strong. Real click-through rates. Tim Ferriss tested "Drug Dealing for Fun and Profit" vs. "The 4-Hour Work Week" this way — the latter won decisively. |
| **Social media poll** | Free | 1-2 days | Moderate. Biased toward your existing audience but useful for directional signal. |
| **Ask 10 target readers** | Free | 1 week | Moderate. In-person reactions are honest. Watch their face, not their words. |
| **Amazon search test** | Free | 5 min | Weak but essential. Search your exact title — if results are cluttered, change it. |

**What to test:**
- Title only (no subtitle) — does it create curiosity?
- Title + subtitle together — does it convert?
- Title on a mock cover at thumbnail size — is it readable?

---

## Step 6: Final Validation

Before locking the title:

- [ ] Search Amazon for exact title — no duplicates in your category
- [ ] Search Google for exact title — no dominant results that will bury you
- [ ] Primary keyword appears naturally in subtitle
- [ ] Title + subtitle total < 80 characters (ideal) or < 200 characters (max)
- [ ] Title works at thumbnail size on the cover
- [ ] Subtitle could stand alone as a clear book description
- [ ] You can say the full title + subtitle in one breath
- [ ] At least one person in your target audience has reacted positively
- [ ] Title does NOT contain: health claims (heal/cure), Amazon program names, brand names, or subjective claims (best/amazing/#1)
- [ ] The free opening chapter slakes the curiosity the title creates AND proves the subtitle's promises (Lisec's "Chapter 1 test")

## Expert Disagreements (Important Context)

Understanding where experts disagree helps you make better tradeoffs:

| Topic | Camp A | Camp B | Resolution |
|-------|--------|--------|------------|
| **Curiosity vs. Clarity** | Lisec, Tucker Max: Title should provoke curiosity, NOT describe contents | Chesson, KDP Kings: Title should contain keywords for search | Curiosity in title + keywords in subtitle. Both are right about their component. |
| **Short vs. Long titles** | Bestseller data: 1-6 syllables dominant | Some KDP publishers use full-keyword titles ("How to Train Your Border Collie") | Short titles for authority/brand books. Keyword titles for pure KDP niche books with no author platform. |
| **When to title** | Lisec: Subtitle first, title last | Most authors: Title first | Lisec is right for strategic/authority books. For KDP niche books, keyword research comes first, then subtitle, then title. |
| **Testing** | Tucker Max: Brainstorm for months, test extensively | KDP Kings: Ship fast, iterate | Depends on stakes. $5 KDP book = ship fast. Career-defining authority book = test extensively. |
| **Cleverness** | Tucker Max: Smart wordplay can work (examples: "Freakonomics") | Chesson: Clear beats clever for discoverability | Cleverness works only when the subtitle does the explaining. Never clever + vague. |

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: KDP Niche Book (Meal Prep)

**Step 1 — 3 Promises:**
- Generic functional: Save time on weeknight cooking
- Specific functional: 30-minute Sunday prep sessions
- Generic emotional: Freedom from daily "what's for dinner" stress

**Step 2 — Keywords:** "meal prep for beginners" (8K monthly searches), "easy meal prep" (5K)

**Step 3 — Title candidates:**
1. *The Sunday Prep* — Concept (4 syllables) ✓
2. *Cook Once, Eat All Week* — Promise (6 syllables) ✓
3. *Meal Prep Made Simple* — Descriptive + keyword (5 syllables) ✓

**Step 4 — Subtitle:** "30-Minute Meal Prep for Beginners: Simple Recipes to Save Time, Eat Healthy, and End Dinnertime Stress"

**Evaluation:** Option 2 scores 9/10 (loses 1 point: no keyword in title, but subtitle covers it).

### Example 2: Authority Book (Leadership)

**Step 1 — 3 Promises:**
- Generic functional: Lead teams through uncertainty
- Specific functional: The 3-conversation framework
- Generic emotional: Confidence in high-stakes moments

**Step 3 — Title candidates:**
1. *The Three Conversations* — Concept (5 syllables) ✓
2. *Steady Under Fire* — Metaphor (5 syllables) ✓
3. *Lead Through Anything* — Promise (4 syllables) ✓

**Step 4 — Subtitle:** "A Leader's Framework for Making Decisions, Building Trust, and Staying Calm When Everything's on the Line"

## Workflow Summary

```
1. Define 3 promises (generic functional, specific functional, emotional)
2. Research keywords (Amazon autocomplete + Publisher Rocket + competitor mining)
3. Draft 5-10 subtitle candidates (weave promises + keywords)
4. Draft 10-15 title candidates (use 9 title types as prompts)
5. Score against 10-point checklist
6. Test top 2-3 with PickFu or ads
7. Run final validation checklist
8. Lock title + subtitle
```
