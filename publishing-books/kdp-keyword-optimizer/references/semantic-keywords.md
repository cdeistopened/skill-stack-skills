# Semantic Keywords and the A10 Algorithm

How Amazon's search has evolved and what it means for keyword strategy.

---

## The Shift: From Exact Match to Semantic Relevance

### Before (A9 Algorithm)
- Exact keyword matching dominated
- More keywords = more visibility
- Keyword stuffing was somewhat effective
- Repetition could help rankings

### Now (A10 Algorithm + Rufus AI)
- **Semantic relevance** is prioritized
- Amazon asks: "Does this book match the *intent* of the search?"
- Quality of match matters more than quantity of keywords
- Context and meaning trump exact words

---

## What Is Semantic Search?

Semantic search understands **meaning**, not just words.

**Example search:** "lighthearted beach read for a mom who needs a break"

**Old approach:** Look for exact words—"lighthearted," "beach," "mom"

**Semantic approach:** Understand the concept—relaxing, escapist fiction, low-stress, feel-good, probably romance or women's fiction

**Implication:** Your keywords should describe the *experience* and *feeling* of your book, not just topics.

---

## Amazon's Rufus AI

Rufus is Amazon's generative AI shopping assistant (launched 2024).

### How Rufus Works
- Scans your entire listing (title, description, reviews, keywords)
- Builds a contextual understanding of your book
- Matches books to conversational queries

### What Rufus Looks For
- **Themes** extracted from reviews and description
- **Reader sentiment** (how people feel about the book)
- **Use cases** (who buys this and why)
- **Comparable products** (what else these readers buy)

### Implication for Keywords
Your keywords should reinforce themes that appear in your:
- Book description
- Editorial reviews
- Customer reviews

**Consistency matters.** If your keywords say "action thriller" but reviews say "slow burn romance," Rufus gets confused.

## Three Pillars of A10 Relevance

### 1. Semantic Relevance
Does the book actually match search intent?

**How to optimize:**
- Use keywords that describe the reading experience
- Include emotional outcomes ("feel good," "page turner")
- Match the language your readers use in reviews

### 2. External Traffic Quality
Are you bringing customers to Amazon from outside sources?

**What counts:**
- Social media clicks to your book page
- Email list traffic
- Blog/podcast referrals
- TikTok/BookTok mentions

**Implication:** Keywords help Amazon understand what your book is about, but external traffic signals that real people want it.

### 3. Sales Velocity + Conversion
Do people who find your book actually buy it?

**Conversion signals:**
- Click-through rate on search results
- Time spent on product page
- Add-to-cart and purchase rate

**Implication:** Keywords that attract the *wrong* readers hurt you. Specificity > volume.

## Keyword Strategy for Semantic Search

### DO: Use Conceptual Phrases

| Instead of... | Use... |
|---------------|--------|
| "mystery book" | "cozy whodunit amateur sleuth small town" |
| "self help" | "personal transformation mindset shift" |
| "romance" | "slow burn enemies to lovers second chance" |
| "fasting" | "spiritual discipline catholic lent devotional" |

### DO: Include Reader Outcomes

What will the reader **feel** or **gain**?

- "feel good summer read"
- "page turner cant put down"
- "learn to meditate beginner"
- "overcome anxiety practical"

### DO: Match Review Language

Read your reviews (or competitor reviews). What words do readers use?

If readers say: "This helped me finally understand..."
Keyword: "finally understand [topic] explained simply"

### DON'T: Stuff Synonyms

**Old thinking:** "fasting fast fasts fasted intermittent"
**New thinking:** "intermittent fasting spiritual discipline" (one coherent phrase)

---

## Testing Semantic Relevance

### The Rufus Test
Ask yourself: "If a reader described my book to a friend, what would they say?"

That description = your keywords.

### The Review Mining Method
1. Read 10-20 reviews of your book (or closest comp)
2. Note recurring phrases and descriptors
3. Use those exact phrases as keywords

**Why this works:** You're using the language real readers use, which is what Rufus learns from.

---

## Long-Tail Phrases for Semantic Search

Long-tail phrases (4-6 words) work better than short phrases because they capture intent.

| Short (weak) | Long-tail (strong) |
|--------------|-------------------|
| "diet book" | "keto diet for women over 40" |
| "mystery" | "clean cozy mystery animal lover" |
| "business" | "startup guide first time entrepreneur" |
| "fasting" | "christian intermittent fasting lent guide" |

**Why long-tail wins:**
- Less competition
- Higher intent (reader knows what they want)
- Better conversion (exact match to need)

## The 7-Slot Semantic Strategy

Distribute your keywords to cover different semantic angles:

| Slots | Focus | Example |
|-------|-------|---------|
| 1-2 | Primary concept + audience | "catholic lent fasting spiritual discipline" |
| 3-4 | Reader outcomes + emotions | "closer to god peace clarity freedom" |
| 5 | Comparable works/authors | "eat fast feast fans jason fung christian" |
| 6 | Specific use cases | "40 day devotional prayer journal ash wednesday" |
| 7 | Category signals | "religious holidays worship meditation" |

## Key Takeaways

1. **Semantic relevance > keyword volume** — Quality beats quantity
2. **Describe the experience** — How will readers feel?
3. **Use reader language** — Mine reviews for real phrases
4. **Long-tail phrases** — 4-6 words capture intent
5. **Consistency across listing** — Keywords should match description and reviews
6. **Rufus reads everything** — Your whole listing matters, not just keywords

---

*Amazon's algorithm is getting smarter. Your keywords should describe what your book IS, not just what topics it covers.*
