# Cold Outreach System

Walk a founder through building a cold outreach engine using Sam Parr's email playbook, Sarah Moore's mass outreach system, and Shaan Puri's creative access strategies from My First Million.

## When to Use

The user needs to reach people they have no connection to — potential customers, investors, partners, mentors, or acquisition targets. They might say:
- "I need to reach [type of person] and I have no connections"
- "How do I cold email effectively?"
- "Help me get meetings with [target audience]"
- "I want to reach out to potential customers/partners"

## The Philosophy

From Sam Parr (`ezFDXJv-baA.md`):

> "Pick 100 people you want to reach. Spend only 30 minutes a day for the next month, and follow up with each of them 10 times until they say 'no thank you' or 'yes.' Your reply rate, if you're any good, is going to be about 10%."

The key insight: most people send one email and give up. The follow-ups are where all the value is.

> "There's the initial email, which a few brave people are willing to do, and then there are the four follow-ups that basically nobody is willing to do."

## Phase 1: Build the Target List

**Step 1: Define your ideal target with specifics**

Don't say "CEOs of mid-size companies." Say "CEOs of SaaS companies doing $2-10M ARR in the HR tech space." The more specific, the better your emails will be.

Ask the user:
- Who exactly do you want to reach? (Title, company size, industry)
- Why would they care about what you're offering?
- What's the ask? (Meeting, sale, advice, partnership)

**Step 2: Build a list of 100 names**

Sources:
- LinkedIn Sales Navigator (filter by company size, title, industry)
- Industry conference speaker lists
- Podcast guest lists (search MFM transcripts for guests in your space)
- "Who are the 10 best companies in this space?" → find their leaders

**Step 3: Find email addresses**

From Sarah Moore's acquisition outreach (`dF6zvTXimxY.md`):

> "Email addresses all follow patterns. You look at them all day: most are first name, last initial at domain — or first initial, last name. We created a big Excel file, guessed the combinations, and ran them through Bulk Email Checker."

Common patterns to try:
- `first@domain.com`
- `first.last@domain.com`
- `firstlast@domain.com`
- `first_last@domain.com`
- `flast@domain.com` (first initial + last name)

Verify with: Hunter.io, Bulk Email Checker, or similar tools.

## Phase 2: Write the Email

**Sam's Rule of Reciprocity approach (`ezFDXJv-baA.md`):**

> "The rule of reciprocity states that when you do something nice for someone, they will almost always do something nice back — often not in proportion to the gift."

Lead with value, not with your ask. Give them something useful before requesting anything.

**Shaan's "Article" technique:**

> "I'd say, 'Hey, I'm doing an article on my favorite five [X] businesses. I want to feature you guys — just need to hop on a call for 30 minutes, ask you a few questions.' And then during that call, while ostensibly doing the article, I'd ask them how they started, how they get leads, what's the hardest part."

This works because:
- You're offering them something (publicity, recognition)
- The questions you need answered for "the article" are the same questions you need for your business
- It positions you as a peer, not a supplicant

**Sam's "Story as Differentiator" principle:**

> "The CEO of Ace Hardware? You will be the most interesting email he gets that day. Nobody else is emailing him."

If you have an unusual story — you're 17, you're from a tiny town, you bootstrapped something weird — lead with it. Decision-makers are drowning in polished pitches. A genuine human story stands out.

**Email structure (3-5 sentences max):**
1. One sentence showing you know who they are (specific, not generic flattery)
2. One sentence on why you're reaching out (the value or the story)
3. One sentence with the ask (specific and small — a 15-minute call, not "pick your brain")

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't write a wall of text
- Don't lead with your credentials
- Don't use "I know you're busy" (everyone says this)
- Don't ask to "pick their brain" (vague and one-sided)

## Phase 3: The Follow-Up System

This is where 90% of people fail and where 90% of results come from.

**The cadence:**
- Day 0: Initial email
- Day 3: Follow-up 1 (bump the original, add one new piece of value or context)
- Day 7: Follow-up 2 (try a different angle — maybe reference something they recently posted)
- Day 14: Follow-up 3 (shorter, more direct — "Just want to make sure this didn't get buried")
- Day 21: Follow-up 4 (the "breakup email" — "I'll assume this isn't a priority right now. If anything changes, I'm here.")

**Sam's 10-follow-up rule:**
Sam says 10 follow-ups. Most people won't do this. But the math works: if your initial email gets a 2% reply rate and each follow-up adds 1-2%, by follow-up 5 you're at 10%+.

**Sarah Moore's counter-intuitive insight:**
> "The people who responded and said, 'You idiot, that's not true' — great. Now we know that."

Negative replies are still replies. They give you information. The only bad outcome is silence — which is why you follow up.

## Phase 4: The Conversation

Once you get the meeting:

**Sam's discovery call approach (from `dL60Qvntw58.md`):**

Anchored by The Mom Test — never ask "would you pay for this?" Instead:
- "How do you currently handle [problem]?"
- "What have you tried?"
- "How much have you spent trying to solve this?"
- "What would the ideal solution look like?"

The answers to these questions tell you whether your outreach target is a real prospect — or just being polite.

**Shaan's "blueprint extraction" technique:**

> "While ostensibly doing the article, I'd ask them how they started, how they get leads, what's the hardest part. I'd try to figure out a blueprint."

Your first few conversations aren't sales calls — they're research. You're figuring out the common patterns: what do these people care about, what language do they use, what's their real problem (not what they say on LinkedIn)?

## Phase 5: Scale What Works

Once you've found the message that gets replies and the conversation that converts:

- Template the winning email (keep it feeling personal)
- Systematize the follow-up cadence
- Track: emails sent, replies, meetings booked, conversions
- Hire a VA to handle the mechanical parts (list building, email verification, scheduling)

**Sam's scaling principle from The Hustle:**
He built The Hustle's initial audience through cold email to conference attendees. The process: attend events → collect business cards → email everyone → repeat. Volume + follow-up = growth.

## Output

Help the user build:
1. **Target list** — 100 specific names with a clear criteria
2. **Email template** — 3-5 sentences, personalized, value-first
3. **Follow-up sequence** — 5 emails over 21 days
4. **Tracking system** — spreadsheet with sent/replied/meeting/converted columns
5. **Discovery questions** — for the first conversation
6. **Scale plan** — what to systematize once the process works
