# Video Caption & On-Screen Hook Writer (v3)

On-screen text is the #1 visual element in short-form video. It does the PRIMARY work of stopping a scroll. A viewer sees the text and decides in <1 second whether to stop. This skill creates the text that makes them stop.

**The only question that matters:** What text would make a parent stop scrolling - without hearing a single word?

Not "what complements the audio." Not "what pattern fits this clip." Just: would this make someone stop?

## The Transformation Rule (Core Framework)

From Andrew Muto's caption optimization work. This is the single most important thing in this skill.

**Every weak hook can be transformed using three moves:**

| From | To | Example |
|------|----|---------|
| Technical/Educational | Emotional/Dramatic | "Fastest way to get kids interested in learning" → "Your kid's Minecraft addiction is actually genius" |
| Long Explanation | Punchy Statement | "Learning from bad experiences" → "My son's first job sucked. Perfect." |
| Inside Baseball | Universal Truth | "Student success story" → "Never give up on the weird kid" |

**The pattern:** Weak hooks describe the content. Strong hooks provoke an emotion. The transformation is always from WHAT THE CONTENT IS ABOUT to HOW IT MAKES SOMEONE FEEL.

### Before/After Swipe File

Study these transformations. They teach you more than any framework:

| Weak (describes content) | Strong (provokes emotion) | Why it works |
|--------------------------|--------------------------|--------------|
| "Adulting 101" | "My son's first job sucked. Perfect." | Unexpected positive reaction to negative outcome |
| "Fastest way to get kids interested in learning" | "Your kid's Minecraft addiction is actually genius" | Reframes parental worry into validation |
| "When schools don't trust parents to know their kids" | "Stop raising kids who wait for permission" | Direct challenge that parents feel in their gut |
| "Important parenting advice" | "Never give up on the weird kid" | Every parent fears their kid is "the weird one" |
| "Student success story" | "Never give up on the weird kid" | Universal emotional truth vs. generic label |
| "The Trojan horse classroom" | "She put 3 grades in one room. Everyone said she was crazy." | Story setup vs. jargon label |
| "'Why' opens the wrong filing cabinet" | "Parents, delete this word from your vocabulary." | Direct command with mystery vs. episode-specific metaphor |
| "5 years to solve a car ride" | "She's a parenting expert. Her own kid broke her." | Relatable human struggle vs. cryptic reference |

### The Acid Test for Every Hook

Ask these three questions in order:

1. **Would someone who's never heard of this episode understand this hook?** If no, you're writing inside baseball.
2. **Does this hook describe the content or provoke an emotion?** If it describes, transform it.
3. **Say the hook out loud to a stranger. Do they lean in or look confused?** Confused = rewrite.

## Three Functions of On-Screen Text

On-screen text isn't decoration. It does three jobs simultaneously (Andrew Muto):

1. **Immediate Context** - Tell viewers what they're watching from frame one. A silent scroller needs to know in <1 second: is this for me?
2. **Drama Injection** - Add tension when the opening audio lacks punch. Most podcast clips start conversationally. The text supplies the urgency the audio doesn't have yet.
3. **Search Optimization** - Help algorithms understand your content. Topic words in the text overlay get indexed alongside the auto-transcribed audio.

**When writing a hook, check it against all three.** A hook that nails drama but gives zero context about the topic will get views but wrong-audience views. A hook that's all context ("Homeschool math tips") has zero stopping power.

## The First-3-Words Test

The first 3 words someone reads do 80% of the work. Before finalizing any hook, check:

**What are the first 3 words?**

- "Your kid's Minecraft" → Instant parent identification
- "Stop raising kids" → Direct challenge, emotional
- "Never give up" → Inspirational + universal
- "55% of teachers" → Specificity (tells me this is data)
- "She's a parenting" → Story setup with credential

**Weak first 3 words:**
- "Here's what happens" → Generic, could be anything
- "Check this out" → Zero information
- "In this clip" → Meta, breaks immersion
- "You need to" → Commanding without earning attention
- "The research shows" → Academic, not emotional

**Front-load the punch.** Move the most surprising, specific, or provocative word as close to position 1 as possible.

## Length Variety (Critical)

**Every set of 3-4 hook options MUST include different lengths.** This is non-negotiable. If all your hooks are 3-5 word punchy phrases, you're in a rut.

| Length | Word Count | When It Works | Example |
|--------|-----------|---------------|---------|
| **Punchy** | 2-5 words | Stats, provocative labels, bold claims | "Homework is a scam" |
| **Statement** | 6-9 words | Reframes, challenges, story setups | "His parents almost cut off his entire career" |
| **Narrative** | 10-15 words | Story setups, scenario hooks, before/after | "She put 3 grades in one room. Everyone said she was crazy." |
| **Full quote** | 15+ words | When the quote itself IS the hook (rare, powerful) | "Kids learn to walk at different ages... so why do we make every kid learn algebra at the same age?" |

**The trap:** AI defaults to short and punchy because it pattern-matches "hook = headline." Fight this. Some of the best-performing on-screen text is conversational and long.

## Hook Categories That Work

### 1. Polarizing Statements
Take something the audience worries about and reframe it as positive, or take something accepted and challenge it.
- "Your kid's [negative thing] is actually genius"
- "Stop raising [undesirable trait] kids"
- "Homework is a scam"
- "Your ADHD kid isn't broken"
- "Your kid's C+ is fine"

### 2. Counter-Intuitive Reveals
Pair a negative with an unexpected positive reaction. The juxtaposition creates the hook.
- "My son's first job sucked. Perfect."
- "Screen time was never the enemy. Boredom was."
- "The worst experiences are the best teachers"

### 3. Direct Challenges
Command the viewer. Works when the challenge validates what they already secretly believe.
- "Never give up on the weird kid"
- "Stop raising kids who wait for permission"
- "Make your kids love learning again"

### 4. Curiosity Gaps
Hint at what happened without revealing it. Story setup that demands completion.
- "She's a parenting expert. Her own kid broke her."
- "His kids skipped school for 100 days. Here's what happened."
- "Finland banned homework below age 10. Now they're #1 in education."

### 5. Identity Hooks
Name the viewer's identity so they self-select in.
- "If you're a [identity], watch this"
- "Things I don't explain to strangers anymore: Why my kids aren't in school"
- "My kids socialize with humans of all ages, not just 24 kids born the same year"

### 6. Data Punches
Lead with a specific number. Specificity = credibility = stopping power.
- "55% of teachers want to leave the profession"
- "Edison was kicked out of school for being 'too curious'"
- "5 Signs Your Kid Doesn't Actually Hate Math"

## Podcast Clip On-Screen Hook Workflow

Step-by-step process for generating hooks from podcast transcript clips. Used by the podcast-production skill at Step 4.

### Step 1: Find the Emotional Core

Read the clip transcript and ask: **What's the "quiet part" we can say out loud?**

Look for (SURP framework):
- **S**urprising fact or statistic ("55% of teachers want to leave")
- **U**nexpected quote or bold statement ("you've lost the argument")
- **R**elatable problem ("kids feel lost, so they retreat to the screen")
- **P**rovocative opinion ("our path to victory will be to trap families")

The hookable moment is usually NOT the first sentence of the clip. Find the single line that would make someone stop scrolling if they saw it as a headline.

### Step 2: Transform It

Take whatever you found and run it through the Transformation Rule:

- **Is it technical/educational?** → Make it emotional/dramatic
- **Is it a long explanation?** → Compress to a punchy statement
- **Is it inside baseball?** → Find the universal truth underneath

**The question to ask:** "A parent scrolling Instagram at 10pm, sound off, has never heard of this podcast. What text makes them stop and tap?"

NOT: "What text complements the audio?" That's a useful secondary consideration, but it's not the starting point.

### Step 3: Generate 3-4 Hook Options

For each clip, write 3-4 options with LENGTH VARIETY. Each set should span at least 2 different length categories (punchy, statement, narrative, full quote).

Scan the Hook Categories and Proven Hook Library for a matching pattern. Adapt the pattern to this specific content while preserving the format's proven structure.

**Warning signs you're writing weak hooks:**
- Uses jargon or vocabulary from the episode
- Explains rather than intrigues
- Generic phrases anyone could write about any clip
- Requires episode context to understand
- Describes the content instead of provoking an emotion

### Step 4: Quality Gates

For each option, check:
- [ ] **McDonald's Test:** Someone at McDonald's understands instantly?
- [ ] **First-3-Words Test:** First 3 words carry the punch?
- [ ] **Scroll Test:** Would this make ME stop scrolling in my feed?
- [ ] **Grandmother Test:** Does this hint at the payoff without giving it away?

### Step 5: Mark Recommended Pick

Select one recommended option and explain WHY in one sentence. The rationale should reference what makes it stop a scroll - not how it relates to the audio.

## Audio-Text Relationship (Secondary Principle)

Once you have a hook that stops the scroll on its own, THEN consider how it works with the audio. This is the old "complementarity principle" - still valid, just not the starting point.

**Good pairing:** Text creates a frame, audio fills it in. The gap between what you read and what you hear creates curiosity.

| If the audio... | The text should... |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Makes a bold claim | Set up the stakes ("She's a parenting expert. Her own kid broke her.") |
| Tells a story | Hint at the ending without revealing it |
| Cites data/stats | Frame why the data matters to the viewer |
| Gives advice | Name the problem the advice solves |

**The test:** Does reading the text AND hearing the audio create a fuller picture than either alone? If the text just labels what the audio says, it's wasted real estate.

## Identifying Hookable Moments in Transcripts

When scanning a full transcript (before clips are selected), look for:

**Tier 1: Almost always hookable**
- Specific statistics ("55% of teachers want to leave")
- Moments that contradict popular belief
- Emotional peaks (speaker's voice changes, pace quickens)
- Universal pain points ("every parent knows...")
- Confessions from experts ("I'm a parenting expert and I lost it on my kid")

**Tier 2: Often hookable**
- Personal confessions or vulnerable moments
- "I should probably not say this, but..."
- Origin stories (how they started, what went wrong)
- Direct statements that take a side

**Tier 3: Rarely hookable (avoid)**
- Agreement moments ("yeah, totally, I think so too")
- Background context / scene-setting
- Nuanced, heavily-caveated statements
- Jargon-heavy industry talk
- Named concepts/frameworks that only make sense with explanation

**The whole transcript matters.** The strongest moment might be at minute 48. Don't mine just the first half.

## The Triple Word Score System

Four signals must align so algorithms AND humans immediately know "this is for me":

### 1. Audio Transcript (MOST IMPORTANT)
- What the speaker says out loud - algorithms auto-transcribe this
- Topic words must appear in first 10 seconds
- Core terminology repeated naturally throughout

### 2. On-Screen Text Hook
- Visual overlay that stops the scroll (everything above)
- NOT a repetition of the audio - adds a new frame
- Lead with topic words when possible

### 3. Caption Copy
- Post description with topic-relevant keywords
- Opens with a topic-relevant phrase
- Provides context the algorithm needs

### 4. Strategic Hashtags
- 10-12 total (optimal range)
- Broad → Mid → Specific → Niche → Audience → Platform
- Example: #Education #Parenting #Homeschool #Microschools #SchoolChoice #HomeschoolMom #Shorts

**When all four align, the algorithm recognizes the topic immediately and serves it to the right people.**

## Caption Writing

### Short-Form Platforms (Same Caption Everywhere)

**Applies to:** YouTube Shorts, Instagram Reels, TikTok, Facebook Reels

One caption per clip. Don't write platform-specific versions (waste of time, same audience).

**Format:**
```
[Verbatim quote or key insight from clip - 1-2 sentences]
[Context: who the speaker is and why it matters - 1 sentence]

#Hashtags (10-12)
```

**Optional X Variant:**
Shorter, more conversational. Include @handles. Drop the context sentence.

### Caption Quality Check
- [ ] Opens with the strongest line from the clip
- [ ] Includes guest name and title
- [ ] Hashtags span broad to niche
- [ ] Under 150 characters for the hook portion
- [ ] No emojis in body (rare exceptions for social captions)

## Common Mistakes

1. **Correlative constructions.** "Your kid isn't X, they're Y" / "Kids don't need X, they need Y" / "Not X, but Y." This is the #1 AI tell. Find another way. Say one side of it and let the viewer fill the gap. "Delete this word" beats "Stop using this word and use this other word instead."
2. **Writing about the episode instead of for the viewer.** "Why vs What" is meaningless to someone who hasn't watched. "One word shuts kids down. The other makes them think." is universal.
2. **All hooks the same length.** If every option is 3-5 word punchy headlines, you haven't explored the full range.
3. **Describing content instead of provoking emotion.** "Parenting expert shares advice" describes. "She's a parenting expert. Her own kid broke her." provokes.
4. **Using episode-specific metaphors.** If the guest coined a term or used a metaphor, it won't mean anything to someone who hasn't heard the episode. Find the universal truth underneath.
5. **Giving away the payoff.** If the text reveals the full insight, there's no reason to watch.
6. **Generic first 3 words.** "Here's what happens" could be anything. "Your kid's Minecraft" is specific.
7. **Fancy vocabulary.** "Pedagogical paradigm shift" fails the McDonald's test. "Schools are broken" passes.
8. **Starting with the audio-text relationship.** Start with "would this stop a scroll?" THEN consider how text and audio work together.
